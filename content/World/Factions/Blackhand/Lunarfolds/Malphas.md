## Description

A Lunarian man with black wings and feathers that spark with barely-contained electricity. His body thrums with the power of the Mythical Zoan: Thunder Bird. Hair matted, wings like a half-closed sail — he carries himself with the weight of everything he's lost and the defiance of someone who refuses to stop.

## Role

Lunarian warrior and member of the [[Lunarfolds]]. Thunder Bird Zoan user. Sails under [[Blackhand Cane]] after being rescued by [[Baptiste]] at a black-market auction.

Devil Fruit: [[Tori Tori no Mi — Model Raicho]].

## Personal Quests

- Find his sister **Alice**, taken during the attack on their Lunarian village
- Uncover who ordered the raid — figures in white coats bearing the symbol of Libra

## Backstory

### Alice's POV — The Village Falls

![[Attachments/Alice.png]]

Morning reached the Lunarian village gently, the way it always did—light spilling like molten gold over the high plateau, brushing the stone rooftops and silver-feathered banners that fluttered lazily in the wind. The sun rose close here, closer than anywhere else in the world; the village was perched at the very top of the Grand Line, seemingly carved into the heavens themselves.

"Alice," her mother's voice echoed through the small home, firm but not unkind. "Up. The council is gathering."

Alice groaned into her blankets. She was twelve, and mornings were the enemy. "I'm awake… mostly," she mumbled.

Her mother, Chieftain Blerta, leaned over her. Proud black wings folded neatly behind her, sunlight haloing her stern face. "You are my heir. You don't have the luxury of lazy mornings."

Alice sat up, rubbing her eyes, hair sticking in every direction. "I know, I know. I just… I liked it better when I wasn't an heir."

"That is not how inheritance works," Blerta said, though Alice could hear a smile behind the words.

Outside, the village was alive. Warriors sparred in courtyards, children chased each other between looming sky-torches, and elders tended to gardens that grew along the cliff edges. Alice followed her mother to the council hall—a carved dome of obsidian and white skywood—its doors already open.

Inside, voices were tense.

"The caravans from Sea Gate haven't returned in three days."

"We've seen lights on the horizon—metallic, green-glowing. Not ships we recognise."

"And scouts report masked men near the lower paths."

Blerta raised a hand. Silence fell.

"Until we know more," she said, "we prepare. Quietly. No panic."

Alice watched them all. Every voice felt heavy, wary. She sucked in a breath—this wasn't the usual talk about border disputes or harvest allocations. Something was wrong. Very wrong.

After the meeting, Alice shifted restlessly. "Mother… are we in danger?"

"All lands are always in danger." Blerta rested a hand briefly on her shoulder. "But ours stands tall. Go to training. Show me you understand your duties."

Alice joined the women warriors atop the sunlit terrace. Their black wings flashed like obsidian as they drilled forms. Alice, though young, moved with natural grace. Her punches were clean, her stance steady. The warriors whispered proudly—"She'll surpass her mother someday." Alice tried to hide her smile every time she overheard it.

After training, she rushed down the sloping path toward the fields. Malphas was there, as always, sleeves rolled up, digging through the soil with disciplined focus. Her older brother was quiet, patient, always thinking before he spoke—everything Alice was not.

She flopped down beside him. "Council meeting was boring."

Malphas chuckled softly. "I'm sure Mother will be glad to hear that."

"You're supposed to agree with me."

"I'm supposed to be the responsible one."

"That's my job now."

"Well," Malphas said, setting down his tools, "then you might want to stop throwing pebbles at my head."

Alice froze mid-throw. "Oh."

He flicked the pebble from her hand. "Come. Father should be at the gatehouse by now."

They walked together toward the towering gate carved into the mountain's edge. Their father Kreshnik stood watch, spear in hand, wings casting a long shadow. He greeted them with a warm nod. "My stars. Both of you together—should I be worried?"

"Always," Alice said proudly.

He ruffled her hair. "Then I am prepared."

Evening dipped toward night. Lanterns lit the pathways, families ate together, music drifted from the hearth-hall. It felt peaceful. Normal.

Until it didn't.

A thundercrack split the air.

Alice jerked upward. Malphas froze. Their father took one step outside the gatehouse—and the sky tore open.

Three figures descended from above, walking on the air itself. Boots clicking on invisible steps. Coats whipping. One wore swirling white. Two others in sharp armoured suits—♾️.

The first villager screamed.

The attack began.

Energy blasts streaked downward, ripping through rooftops. Warriors leapt into the air but were shot from the sky. The three intruders moved like falling stars—fast, precise, merciless.

"Malphas!" their father roared. "Take your sister. RUN."

Malphas grabbed Alice's hand. She had never seen him scared. Not like this. They sprinted between shattered houses as the ground shook and fire licked the sky.

Behind them, adults were falling—elders, warriors, their mother's council guards. Alice's breaths came fast, sharp; her wings trembled violently.

They reached a narrow alley—then a dozen figures in white coats and gas masks slid from the shadows like ghosts.

"Targets confirmed. The Lunarian children," one said, voice flat.

Malphas stepped in front of Alice, fists clenched. "Stay behind me."

The masked men lunged. Malphas fought with desperate fury—punches cracking helmets, feet launching soldiers back. Alice swung wildly, cutting through two with the sharp edge of her wing. But there were too many.

A cannon round blasted from behind the attackers.

Alice didn't see where it came from. She only saw the explosion swallow Malphas.

"NO!" she screamed as he was hurled across the stone.

Her pulse thundered. Rage, fear, grief twisted together. Something ancient roared awake inside her.

A wave burst out of her—a storm of will itself.

White coats collapsed instantly. Helmets cracked. Bodies hit stone. Even the torches flickered as if bowing.

Alice stood trembling, tears streaking down her face.

Then—

A hand clamped around her mouth.

A whisper slid into her ear:

"So the girl possesses the Color of the Supreme King. How… interesting."

A pressure struck the back of her head.

Darkness swallowed her whole.

### Chapter 2 — Cline "The Plague"

![[Cline 1.png]]

The sea had teeth that night. It tore at the wood and tossed the bodies like toys. Cline came to with salt in his mouth and the taste of iron on his tongue, the horizon a blur of black and pale foam. For a long, foggy second he believed the world was still water. Then rough hands hauled him over a rail and the deck lurched under him, throwing him face-first onto wet planks. He coughed until his ribs burned and the world steadied enough for him to see the shapes—two figures in masks leaning over him, their boots slick with seawater.

"Shouldn't we just let him die?" one of them said, the words swallowed by the hiss of their respirator.

"Why not?" the other answered like it was a comfortable choice. "We've cages to fill. He looks like he'll fetch a coin."

Cline tried to make his arms obey. He tried to curl, to make himself nothing. He had practiced this smallness his whole life—making himself small when the looks came, shrinking behind bales and husks of towns so strangers wouldn't see the eyes. But the guards were efficient; they hauled him below, dragging his wet feet across the boards, and shoved him into a narrow cell with a clang of iron. Water dripped from his hair and pooled at his feet. The light in the cell was thin and smelled of oil and old metal.

Across from him, behind a second barred window, a figure sat with his back to the wall. Cline could not yet make out the features—just a silhouette, the slack of someone who had been held too long. He saw, later, how the moonlight touched the other man's shoulders like a promise. For now he only knew the other prisoner breathed shallow and cautious, and a guard outside called, "Hey, Freak—wake up. We move you to your sister at dawn. Boss wants answers."

The word "Freak" had always been a rope around Cline's neck. He had been chased from one harbor to the next at that name. Children had thrown stones at him until he curled into a ball and the town dogs refused him shelter. He had tried to be useful—carrying water, mending nets, fixing shutters—only to be blamed when storms came, as if the sky itself hated him. The memory of one harbor boy's fist, the gleam of fear in a woman's eyes as she crossed to the other side of the lane, lived in him like a fossil. He had learned, painfully, that kindness was a currency he could not spend.

So when the guard beyond the bars leaned close to the other prisoner and mimicked kindly tones—"You'll be singing for us soon enough, friend. Don't be shy"—Cline felt something that was not merely sympathy. It was kinship and a corrosive knot of old shame. He pounded the iron with his fist. "Stop," he called. His voice was small; the guard laughed and poked him with the butt of a baton.

"What are you doing?" the guard asked. "You want to be last for the crate? Quiet, freak."

Cline backed away even as another part of him moved forward—an animal reflex to protect what could not be protected. He had spent years letting himself take the brunt, letting other hands hit him first to spare a startled child or a stranger's pride. It was a coward's logic and a martyr's heart tangled into one. "Please," he whispered, more a plea to the damp air than to the man. "Don't—don't hurt him."

That was when the rod came down. It arced through the cell like a white snake. Cline felt the first strike in his shoulder and the sound of it was a small, traitorous thing—an electric pop that traveled through bone and made his teeth rattle. He cried out, not for himself but for the other prisoner, who flinched as if hearing the same fear. The guard sneered. "You'd get yourself killed for nothing? Pathetic."

Another strike. Pain lanced across his chest. Cline clutched the iron bars and tasted bile. His skin went prickly, as if a swarm of insects skittered beneath it. The thing inside him, the thing he had learned to name only in the loneliest hours—Shadow, Curse, the Other—shifted and grew awake. It had always been there, in tremors and whispers: when a crowd hissed at him, a third eye of feeling opening somewhere behind his ribs. He had managed it by shrinking, by swallowing the urge to lash out. Tonight, under the battering of rods and the smell of salt and iron, control frayed.

"Stop it," he begged and meant it for both the guards and whatever lived beneath his skin. "No more. Please—"

The dark answered like a mouth opening. It unfurled not with noise but with a pressure, a soundless tidal pull that tugged at the edges of the world. At first it was only a shadow at the corner of his vision, like smoke shaped into intent. Then it moved with terrible purpose, long and sinewy, seeping from his joints and through the weave of his clothes. Eyes, wet and glassy and alien, bloomed along his forearms and the inside of his elbow—dozens of them, blinking in a rhythm that tasted of old grief. The first guard's laugh cut off in a short, strangled gasp.

"W—what is that—" the second guard began, but there was no time to finish. Something like a blade formed from the shadow, sharp as despair, and it slid across the deck with surgical grace. The first man's body was bisected with a sound that was all wrong for the ocean: a clean, wet clap, and red geysering sent a fine rain over the planks. Time stuttered as the shadow retracted and a part of Cline felt, absurdly, embarrassment for the mess. Another part was flooded with release—an animal's delighted relief at being unchained.

Terrified shouts ricocheted below decks as men scrambled. Lanterns tipped; boots slapped. The second guard reached for his own weapon and a tentacle of darkness smashed into his throat, cracking bone like a brittle twig. He landed with the wind knocked out of him, eyes popping and white. Blood painted the stripes of the hull. For a breathless moment the ship was a strange new weather: rain of red, a sky of shadow, and Cline at the center of both, fingers curled uselessly around empty air.

He did not mean to open the other cell. He did not mean to tear the lock free, to fling metal aside as if it were paper. He had meant only to stop them, to make them stop and listen. But the shadow did not ask permission. It shoved him, pushed and pulled and used his limbs like ropes. The bars gave with a shriek. The other prisoner—Cline saw him now plainly: young, hair matted, wings like a half-closed sail—was knocked backward by a wave of wind as the hatch exploded open.

"Run!" Cline heard, or thought he heard, but the voice was distant and not fully his. He saw the young man—Malphas—stagger to his feet, wide-eyed, and run for the broken steps that led to the deck. He did not see the speed or the grace he would later claim; he felt only the surge of sky under that figure's feet as he launched, driven by desperation. The shadow made a reach and struck at the ankles, but Malphas pushed through the air like a boy surviving a storm and began to rise.

Around Cline everything was wrong and yet terribly precise: a face the color of wet ash, an arm sliced clean off and still twitching, a guard who had tried to hide behind a column now rolled into the gloom and did not rise. The shadow took meat and bone with a careful, indifferent hunger, and Cline, trapped inside, watched as much as he felt: the eyes on his skin blinking, tentacles unfurling and coiling, a blackness that smelled faintly of the deep sea and of old ironies.

When the last of the men stopped moving, the shadow pulled back as if sated. The world went silent in a way Cline had never known: no footfalls, no ragged breathing, only the drip of seawater from disrupted lanterns. He was spent—hollowed out, as if the light that defined him had been squeezed and wrung and thrown aside. He tried to speak and found only a cracked whisper. "I—" His voice broke. Guilt rolled over him in waves thicker than the sea. He had wanted to be the kind of man who took the blows and kept a clean conscience. Instead he had become what people had always feared. The guards' blood felt too much like proof.

They were both gone—escapees and wreckage. Malphas was a shadow against the sky, flinging himself into the night and away from the smoking corpse of the ship. Cline watched him go, and among the cacophony of emotions something small and stubborn coalesced: relief. It was a shameful relief edged with a stab of longing, the twisted thing that had always ruled him. He had saved someone else, and in saving he had paid the bill the world demanded from him.

When he woke he was on a plank, the rim of it creaking in cold water. His skin felt stretched and foreign; the eyes that had covered his arms were gone, leaving pale scars like the lines of dried riverbeds. The ship that had once been a living thing was only ruins—a splintered skeleton smoking slowly in the moonlight. Somewhere far above, a dark shape cut across the sky and vanished into cloud. Cline tried to move and found his limbs heavy and clumsy, as if someone else had used them and forgotten to return the blueprints.

"Hey—hey you!" a voice called, closer now. Metal gleamed in the distance—white hull, blue pennant. A marine cutter had appeared as if conjured; its crew shouted and tossed ropes. Cline squinted into the lantern glow and saw men in uniform hauling him up. They checked him like an animal, hands quick and clinical. "You're alive. Keep him warm," one barked.

Cline let them. He let them lift him and wrap a frayed blanket around his shoulders. He closed his eyes and tried to fix the last image of Malphas in his mind: the tilt of his head as he rose, a hint of defiance in his shoulders, the thin line of sorrow that had crossed his face when he looked back. Cline thought, absurdly, of apology—he mouthed the word once, silent on the plank, a promise to a boy already far away.

The cutter pulled them toward the hold of the marine ship and the world turned bureaucratic: questions asked in sharp tones, a ledger scratched, bandages tied. Cline lay under a blanket and watched the stars, feeling like a planet with a scarred crust. The shadow inside him was quiet now, retreated like a tide. But the memory of its teeth and the taste of his own hands in blood would never truly fade. He had been the weapon and the wound both.

As the ship cut into the dark sea, Cline's last clear thought before sleep took him was neither pride nor triumph. It was older and quieter: I don't want to hurt anyone. I never wanted to be this. If I can, if I still can, I will carry the pain myself. I will take what the world wants to throw away so others can breathe.

But the ocean keeps records, and it cradles nothing perfectly. The plank underneath him rocked and creaked. Somewhere, far above the clouds, a shadow had wings.

### Chapter 3 — Malphas "The Waning Fist"

Malphas didn't remember the fall.

He remembered the sky folding beneath him, wings faltering, blood roaring in his ears as exhaustion gouged him from the inside. He remembered Cline's monstrous shadow ripping through the ship. Then—

Nothing.

He smashed through the canopy of a tropical island like a meteor with no flame. Branches shattered, stone cracked, and the world swallowed him in a crater of steaming earth. The impact tore consciousness from him like a thief plucking a coin.

For three days, he did not wake.

The islanders found him first. Hunters with feathers braided in their hair approached the crater cautiously. They took one look at the black wings, the charred feathers, the Lunarian markings—and dropped to their knees.

They whispered a single word in their dialect:

"Aru-Ka'to."
The Thunder God Returned.

They carried his limp body to their village, a ring of huts beneath thunderclouds permanently hovering above the plateau. At night, lightning arced with unnatural precision, as though guarding something sacred. They laid Malphas on woven mats, cleaned his burns, wrapped his chest, fed him broth through trembling hands—terrified yet reverent.

But Malphas's internal injuries were catastrophic. His pulse flickered weakly, his lungs burned like crushed embers. The healers whispered among themselves: even gods die.

So they gambled.

From a shrine carved into stone, guarded by seven warriors, they retrieved a radiant fruit—jagged, feathered, glowing faintly blue with threads of lightning crawling across its surface.

The Mythical Zoan: Thunder Bird.

They peeled it reverently, and one drop of its juice touched Malphas's tongue.

Lightning erupted.

His back arched violently. Sparks danced across his skin. His feathers shimmered, every vein humming with a deep electric thrum. Storm clouds above gathered as though sucking in breath. When he gasped awake, the world itself seemed to exhale in relief.

The healers bowed so low their foreheads scraped the ground.

"You… soared again," one whispered, voice trembling.

Malphas blinked, disoriented. His body felt different—lighter, sharper, thrumming with barely-contained energy. His heart beat twice, then once, then twice again—unstable, but powerful.

"I… need to go," he rasped. "My sister… she—"

But the island did not give him time.

The first explosion shook the treeline. The second leveled a hut. Screams rose as masked figures moved through the smoke like ghosts—two of them, marked with the symbol of Libra, the balanced scales etched onto their jackets.

Malphas staggered to his feet, wings flaring open. "Stay behind me!" he shouted to the villagers.

Too late.

The bulky one—massive arms, reinforced armour—slammed his blades into Malphas's ribs before he could react. The smaller one—lanky, flexible, eyes like needles—appeared behind him and clamped a seastone cuff around his wrist.

Malphas roared, lightning spasming from his fingertips, but the seastone drained him instantly. His knees hit the ground. His vision blurred.

"One Lunarian," the lanky one hissed, "freshly awakened Zoan. Maybe we should ask for more for this one."

And the world went black again.

### The Black Market of Krake's Maw

Malphas woke to chains biting into his wrists and ankles. His wings were bound with thick bands of iron. The cage surrounding him sparkled faintly with seastone dust woven into the bars. Every breath felt like he was choking on sand.

He couldn't move.
Couldn't ignite.
Couldn't even stretch his wings.

He looked up. Lantern light struck his face.

He was on a raised platform. An auction stage.

Hundreds watched him—pirates, bandits wearing smiles like cuts in their faces.

The auctioneer's voice dripped with sleaze. "Our next item—rare! Dangerous! A genuine Lunarian with a mythical zoan! Starting bid—"

A figure in the front row lifted his head slightly.

White garb.
Gold-and-onyx mask.
Still. Watching Malphas with a focus so sharp it looked painful.

Malphas felt uncertainty flicker inside him. The masked man's fists were clenched so tight the knuckles bled through the glove.

Before the bidding could continue, the two Shadows pushed through from behind the stage.

"Where are the special guests," the bulky one growled.

"Did you remember to send the invites?" the lanky one sighed.

The masked man tilted his head. "He looks half-dead."

"So?" snapped the lanky one. "He's valuable."

The masked man responded calmly, voice edged with disdain. "Not that valuable."

The bulky one snarled and grabbed Malphas by the hair, dragging him forward until Malphas's forehead hit the cage bars with a metallic clang.

"Maybe this will lower the price," he said.

The first stab came without warning.

Steel slid under Malphas's ribs. He gasped, breath escaping in a ragged choke. The second stab punched into his thigh. A third scraped bone.

Pain flared through him—heatless, powerless.

The masked man's fists trembled.

Malphas hissed through his teeth and slammed his head upward, cracking the Shadow's jaw with a vicious headbutt. Blood sprayed. The bulky one stumbled back, roaring.

The entire auction hall froze.

Then something shifted in the masked man.

A pressure rippled through the crowd—an aura thick, oppressive, dark.

He stood.

The lanky Shadow moved first. "Sit down, mask-boy," he spat. "Or you die next."

A chain shot forward like a whip, aimed at the masked man's throat.

The masked man didn't flinch.
Didn't dodge.
Didn't need to.

Because the chain never touched him.

A black arm, shaped like smoke-solid muscle, erupted from the air behind him and caught the chain mid-snap. A second voidlimb appeared. Then a third. All writhing behind him like silent, obedient beasts.

The hall erupted in panic.

"DEVIL FRUIT USER!"

"GET BACK!"

"WHAT IS HE?!"

The bulky Shadow charged with a roar, swinging a fist the size of a boulder. Malphas, still against the bars, reached desperately for strength, for flame, for lightning—but the seastone drained everything.

He could only watch.

The masked man stepped into a boxer's stance, void-arms aligning behind him like wings of shadow.

"You've hurt him enough," he said quietly.

Then he moved.

A black arm shot past him and struck the bulky Shadow's ribs—crack.
Another coiled around the lanky Shadow's leg—snap.
A third slammed into the ground, launching the masked man forward like a bullet.

Malphas felt the air vibrate with each hit.

The pirates ran for the doors, screaming. Chairs shattered. The ceiling shook.

The bulky one managed to grab Malphas's cage and yank it down, trying to use it as a shield. Malphas hit the ground hard, seastone ringing in his bones.

Instinct surged.

The bindings cracked.
Lightning danced along the metal.
The bars exploded outward.

Malphas rose to his feet—wings flaring, electricity bleeding from every feather.

He met the bulky one head-on.
Fists like thunder.
Feathers sparking like storm glass.
Every hit carving out the pain, carving out the fear, carving out the memory of his village burning.

The fight was brutal.
Bloody.
Animal.

Eventually, Malphas's strength buckled. He dropped to one knee, chest heaving, vision warping from exhaustion and blood loss.

The masked man finished the fight for him.

He crushed the lanky Shadow's throat between two void-arms.
He drove the bulky one through three stone pillars with a single, brutal punch.

Silence fell.

The masked man stepped over the bodies and knelt beside Malphas.

"You're hurt," he said, voice quiet, steady.

Malphas spat blood. "Who… are you?"

The man hesitated, then lifted his mask.

Black wings.
Burning eyes.
A face carved with old battles and harder years.

"My name is Baptiste," he said.
"Lunarian, like you."

Malphas stared, stunned.

Baptiste extended a hand.
"Come with me. I sail under a man named Blackhand. He finds people the world tries to erase."

Malphas swallowed, heart thudding. "My sister… Alice. I need to find her."

Baptiste nodded once—sharp, certain.
"Then we'll find her together."

Malphas took his hand.

And fate shifted.
