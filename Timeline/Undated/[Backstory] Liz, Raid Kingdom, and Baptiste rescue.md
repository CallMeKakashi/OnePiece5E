## Elizabeth Marrow (Liz and Linn)

Liz stuffed the last of her things into a worn leather bag, her fingers trembling but her resolve firm. The room smelled faintly of formaldehyde and dust — the stench of the mortuary that had become their home. A door slammed open.

“What did you do this time?” Linn barked, breathless, his dark coat half-buttoned.

Liz didn’t look up. “I’ve had enough of this place, Linn. Haven’t you ever thought we’re meant to be more than morticians?”

He stepped closer, voice sharp but weary. “You shouldn’t nose into stuff that doesn’t concern you. Nikolai’s been kind enough to feed us, give us a job. Whatever you went snooping around for—actually, I’d rather not know.”

She turned to him, eyes blazing. “Just because you refuse to see it doesn’t mean it’s not happening. You _know_ this place isn’t meant for good, Linn. Whatever Nikolai tells you — it’s a lie, even if he believes it himself. And if you really want to know, I saw them. The people we’re dissecting. They’re not corpses from the streets — they’re _people_ with families. They call themselves **Minks**. I spoke to one of the captured ones… the ones still alive, at least. I also went to Section REDACTED."

Linn froze. “What!? Why the hell would you do that? Look, even if they are people, that has nothing to do with us. The world outside isn’t kind. We’d have starved to death if it weren’t for Nikolai. Stop—hey, stop—why are you packing my stuff too?”

Liz threw another shirt into the bag and zipped it shut. “Because we’re leaving this place tonight. And we’re freeing everyone they’ve captured. It’s inhumane, Linn. I won’t sit by and let this happen anymore. We’re responsible for every death in this place, even if we never held the scalpel. Once we’re out, we’ll make it right — to humanity, no, to the _world_. We can’t be forgiven, but we can try our damned best.”

Tears welled in her eyes as she shoved the bag into his chest. “And you’re coming with me, you understand?”

He looked down, trembling. “I… I’m not as strong as you, Liz. I’m scared. The world won’t accept people like us… you know that.”

“These powers don’t _have_ to be curses,” she said softly. “We can use them for something good. I saw this kid… he should be about our age. They had him strung up. They tried cutting him, burning him — he didn’t even flinch. That’s not right, Linn. We need to help him. I’m going to free the Minks, and then I’ll use the keycard I swiped for Section REDACTED to free that boy. Meet me at Dock 6-A. Grab this.”

She tossed him another bag and ran toward the door.

“Wait! Shouldn’t I come with you?” Linn called out, voice cracking. “Damn it, why are you so stubborn? Just listen to me for once!”

“YOU KNOW THEY CAN’T CATCH ME EVEN IF THEY FIND ME!” she shouted back, her voice fading down the hall. “I’LL BE FINE, JUST WAIT FOR ME!”

And then she was gone.

Linn stood there, clutching the bag, heart pounding. The smell of death lingered in the air, but for the first time, it was the silence that scared him more.

## Blackhand Cane — Raid Kingdom

The deck of the _Queen Anne's Revenge_ creaked under the weight of a restless sea and a crew restless for answers. Blackhand Cane stood with his back to the rail, one boot planted on a coil of rope, the salt spray tasting like iron on his lips. He listened as the murmurs rose and fell like gulls over the water.

"Yer tellin' me she willingly let 'em take her?" Cane's voice was low and threaded with a slow-burning fury—equal parts disbelief and pride.

Tray Vexen, still wiping ale from his beard, spat on the planks and shrugged. "I tried to run with who I could, boss. She wouldn't budge. Told me to run to the old man and say she'd be back in a few weeks. When I got in, they had her chained—strange chains, boss. She couldn't move proper. Cannon fire got Thompson and the rest when we tried to come in." He tossed his mug to the side and clenched his fists; the tremor in his voice betrayed his bravery.

Renzo—first mate, helmsman, and a basking-shark fishman whose jaw could cleave a hull—leaned forward. "This ain't right, Cane. The Navy's footprint there is… heavier'n usual. This reaches higher than a local magistrate. If the Admiralty's pokin' 'round, we can't be drawin' that kind of heat."

Morley, an angelshark fishman with a map scarred across one cheek, rubbed his neck and growled, "We can't leave Mira there. Not like that."

Blackhand Cane let the words settle. He thought of Mira—sharp as a tack, strong fist than most men, and loyal in a way that made fools of better men.

"We can't afford casualties here," he said finally, and his crew heard more than a plan in that sentence—they heard restraint. Tray's shoulders sagged but then straightened again, the stubbornness of youth clashing with the fear under his ribs.

"I can't just leave her, Cap'n. If you don't authorize a raid I'll march there myself, even if it kills me. Sir!" Tray's shout cracked across the deck.

Cane stepped down, the grin that never quite reached his eyes softening as he rested a hand on Tray's shoulder. "She's got a good friend, that one," he rumbled. "But ye misunderstan' me, lad. When I said "minimize casualties," I meant not ours—it's not our blood I'm frettin' over. 'Tis theirs."

Tray blinked, confusion and loyalty warring in his face. Cane's hand tightened for a heartbeat, then loosened. "A fine heart ye've got. Good. We can't have one o' our best die for naught. We raid the island, we fetch Mira back, and any soul stands in our way—well, ye know what to do."

He unrolled the stolen layout that Tray had snatched up, and Cane's finger traced the ink as if tasting the plan. "We don't know exactly where they've put 'er. I'll split ye into teams. Morley, lead Goro and Droven—keep yer heads and don't go moon-feelin' brave."

Droven, snorted. Morley punched him lightly; the gruff affection made the others crack a grin even while the knot of worry grew.

"Renzo," Cane said, "you're with Tray. Find out what they hid and why the Navy's got eyes on this. If there's a ledger, a name, somethin' worth takin', bring it back. But remember—priority's Mira."

Gansai, the spider-crab shipwright with hands like pincers and a mind for rigging, lurked by the mizzen. "I'll hold the ship and the kids safe, Boss. The _Queen Anne's_ won't be takin' on no prisoners without a fight."

Cane's grin returned. "Good. I'll go raise a ruckus in the center—draw most o'their forces like bait to a trap. When I go, make sure the rest o' you strike quick and clean. Tray—you're the heart of this tit-for-tat. Keep her close, and bring her back."

Tray swallowed, eyes bright with unshed tears and the kind of courage that made fools legends. "Aye, Captain."

As the _Queen Anne's Revenge_ set a course toward the island, Cane watched his crew organize with that terrible calm captains learn. He felt the old pull in his chest—the promise he would not break. Steel or no steel, chain or no chain, they would reach into whatever belly of iron or law had swallowed Mira and drag her back.

He spat into the ocean, the salt and the promise mixing. "If they think ta take what's ours, they'll find the sea full o' teeth. Set sail. Raid Kingdom's got a storm comin', an' we're the thunder."

## Baptiste — Germa rescue

Another day of torture passed. They left him to recover from his wounds in a room that smelled of oil and old metal. He chanted the old mantra—half prayer, half plea—"Gods... if we..."—and the thought dissolved in the stale air when something rattled at the heavy steel-reinforced door.

Typically, it took two guards to open this kind of door. This time a girl no older than Baptiste fumbled with the lock and pushed it wide. Her dress matched the others in the facility—plain, heavily patched, and more thread than fabric—but more tattered, as if she had been wrestling with the world and losing. She did not look at him, only around the room, eyes caught by the faint glow of lights embedded in the wood.

She pulled a thin stick from her sleeve and slipped toward the door. Baptiste was still staring when he realized, in a slow, panicked way, that his restraints had been lifted. The strange metal that had covered his body—the cold, permeating layer that had been welded to his skin—was gone.

Shouts rose from the halls: loud, panicked noises mounting from the emptiness outside. "Shit! What are you waiting for? Let's go! Don't tell me you can't even walk!" the girl snapped, then bounded to him and offered her shoulder. "Come on. We should hide. They'll come up here first to look for you. They care about you more than the others. The alarm only rang once—I freed you. I guess those wings are the reason... huh."

Baptiste flung her away. She hit the wall with a sound like a cracked bell. "You're just like them. This is a trap. You'll take me to another steel forest," he spat, furious and trembling.

"Look, I'm not one of them," she said. He watched her hand open; a coiled paper-thin object sprang free and unfurled to gleam in the dim like a blade of moonlight. "See? I'm just as much of a freak. Come. We really have to leave before someone finds us."

He followed—too stunned to refuse. They moved through corridors where shadow swallowed sound. Outside the steel gate the world was an ocean of darkness; when they pressed against a wall the girl spread herself flat against it and, somehow, became part of the color—camouflage, flesh melting into stone. Footsteps stumbled by. Two men marched close enough that their voices punched through the concrete.

When the footsteps faded, she pulled him into another small room and breathed, "We should wait it out here. I've heard more of them are headed this way. Huh, I've never been in this room before." She leaned close; he stumbled back. "Wow—your eyes. Cool."

A voice startled them from deeper shadow. "Hey, you there?"

"Fuck," the girl hissed. She grabbed Baptiste's hand and crouched. "Don't worry—I'm not gonna—" She crawled forward on her knees and revealed herself: from shoulder down she was strung in black chains, a latticework that shadowed her skin. Her hair was red and tangled. Her eyes were—impossibly—emerald green.

"I was just askin' what the ruckus was about. I'm tryin' to figure how much trouble I'm in..." she said with a crooked laugh.

"Aren't you already in trouble? Why'd they tie you like that—were you trying to hurt yourself?" the other girl asked, covering Baptiste with her body as if shields could be made of bone and muscle.

"No. No," the chained woman spat blood and smiled with scars. "People are coming for me. If they've arrived, I have to get away from here." She sounded nervous until an explosion rocked the compound. Brick and glass sighed somewhere outside.

The chained woman's smile went thin. "Now ya wouldn't mind helping a stranger would ya" she said, and then she shouted, eyes frantic. "Cover her, lad! Something's coming!"

He woke to distant yelling and the smell of smoke. A boulder pinned the white-haired girl—one arm sticking from beneath it. "Lift the boulder! Try—Goddammit, didn't she try to save you?" the red haired girl yelled. Baptiste could see the moon through a torn ceiling; the room lay in ruin.

Heat pulsed inside him, a pressure like a fever that gathered and took shape. Wings remembered how to move. They unfurled, clumsy at first then angrily, and he managed—against logic—to shove the boulder aside.

Men in white and blue pointed steel at him. A cannonball slammed into his side and sent him cavorting through the air into a ruined building. The marines surged after him. He scrambled, disoriented by months of captivity; he had no sense of direction. He had failed to save even one person who had been kind. How could he save anyone else?

Bodies thudded. Metal rang out. Pressure pushed down on him until his lungs wanted to quit. From the smoke stepped a towering figure, a man who seemed too big and too calm for the chaos. He carried a blade that would have dwarfed most men; it looked small in his hands. He wore a hat, a brown coat that flapped like a cape, and a grin that never quite left his face.

"What do we have here? Lost child," the man called, turning and scanning the rubble. Someone behind him screamed—then the chained woman, now freed, attacked like a force of nature. She bit, headbutted, and swung chain like a flail. As more men in white and blue assembled behind him he turned. "Put your weapons down and back away—or face my steel." He swung his blade. A storm of wings answered him—Baptiste watched, amazed. People dropped weapons and ran; others charged. The man paused and looked directly at Baptiste.

"Son, I have a question for you..." the man called over the din. "Look behind you—they're running toward you. It didn't work." Baptiste pointed behind the man. "What is it that you want more than anything in the whole world."

Before Baptiste could reply, two figures moved with the sudden precision of predators. From the right a fishman lunged and roared, "Fishman Jujutsu: Basking Bloom!" A curved wall of seawater slammed into the advancing line of marines and swept them off their feet.

From the left another figure leapt and struck like a hammered tide. "Fishman Karate: Silent Seabed Strike!" The water answered, surging up like a fist beneath the marines and knocking them senseless.

A small, wiry kid darted behind Baptiste, leapt up, and in a flurry of teeth and blood bit the head of a marine officer without pause. Another child dove in, wrapping both soldiers in a sticky, green glob that immobilized them and left them choking on their own surprise.

"Boss—Mira! Where are you?" a voice called from the place where the great-faced fishman had struck.

Baptiste asked "Who are you people?"

The answer came with a flag at the edge of the island behind him: "We are the Blackhand Pirates.". as the Jolly Roger flies.

"Just the six of you? This your whole clan? Did they take your family too?" Baptiste asked, curious.

A nearby building groaned and began to give way. The red-haired woman who'd risked herself—Mira—screamed, "Bastards! I'll tear you limb from limb!" She rammed her shoulder against a collapsed wall and shoved it free, chains clattering.

"MIRA! WE ARE HERE! COME THIS WAY!" Cane shouted, voice cutting through the chaos.

"MIRA! WE ARE HERE! COME THIS WAY!" Cane shouted, voice cutting through the chaos.

For a breath she stopped—eyeing Cane—and then, stubborn as iron, she twisted and ran the other way. In two heartbeats a hulking fishman barreled into her from the flank and sent her sprawling; she rolled and scrambled on, fighting as if the world depended on it.

Cane looked to his crew—Renzo, Morley, Tray and the others—and with a quick motion to the towering fishman he sent him bounding off to intercept Mira. The hulking figure crashed into her path, toppling her a short distance away, giving her a chance to breathe and rethink.

Before Cane could speak, Baptiste turned toward the nearest of the marines that still stood, his instincts taking over. He clenched his fists and lunged forward; his movements were heavy and untrained, but desperate. Each punch cracked against steel, and the strange warmth that had been burning in his chest flared with every strike.

As he swung again, a streak of light trailed behind his knuckles—the flame sputtering on and off, licking across his forearms like a pulse that couldn’t decide whether to live or die. He hit another soldier square in the chest, sending him flying back into the rubble. The moonlight spilled through the wreckage above, brushing across Baptiste’s wings, and the flames answered—steady now, glowing gold and white.

Cane watched with a mix of awe and understanding as the boy fought, his strikes wild but fueled by something more than rage. When the last of the marines stumbled away, Cane let out a low whistle, stepping forward through the haze.

“As the chaos burned around them, a gap in the smoke revealed the night sky—the moon full and clear for the first time in his memory. Its light struck his skin, and something deep within him stirred. The heat returned—but this time it didn’t sear. It breathed. The faint shimmer of flame rolled along his wings, soft orange-gold, like the first light of dawn after an endless night.”

Cane saw it and grinned wider. “Heh. Guess the world’s finally baptizin’ you, lad.”

Cane turned back, his grin softer now, and leaned down so that his voice cut through to Baptiste alone. "Now—still haven't answered my question. What is your dream?" His grin was patient, almost kindly, even as smoke and ruin curled around them.

Baptiste felt the question like a hand on his chest. He thought of the girl with emerald eyes, the red-haired savior pinned under stone, the small mercies that had kept him human through months of cruelties.

He looked up at the man whose smile had not faltered even in the teeth of battle. The word rose from somewhere small and stubborn inside him: "Freedom."
